T2 Corporate Tax Return Guide for Small Business (2026)

If your business is incorporated, you have to file a T2 corporate income tax return — every year, whether the company earned a dollar, ran at a loss, or sat completely dormant. That surprises a lot of first-time owner-managers. The T2 is a different animal from the personal T1: more schedules, tighter payment rules, and more places to either save money or trip a penalty. This 2026 T2 corporate tax return guide for small business walks you through who files, when it's due, when the tax is actually payable, the deductions that matter most, and how CCPCs keep their rate low.

Who Has to File a T2?

Every corporation resident in Canada has to file a T2 for every tax year — including non-profits, inactive companies, and corporations that owe no tax. There is no income threshold. If you incorporated a numbered company two years ago and never used it, the CRA still expects a return. The only common exception is a registered charity, which files a T3010 instead.

So even if your corporation was dormant, had no revenue, or lost money, you still file. The good news: a nil or loss year is usually quick, and reporting a loss now can shelter profit in a future year through non-capital loss carry-forwards.

T2 Filing Deadline vs. When the Tax Is Due

These are two separate clocks, and mixing them up is one of the most expensive small-business mistakes we see.

Filing deadline: your T2 is due six months after your fiscal year-end. If your year ends December 31, the return is due June 30. If it ends March 31, it's due September 30.

Payment deadline: any balance owing is due earlier — generally two months after year-end. Many Canadian-controlled private corporations (CCPCs) claiming the small business deduction get three months instead, provided they meet the CRA's conditions (broadly, a CCPC whose taxable income the prior year was within the business limit). So you can file in June but still owe the tax back in February or March — and interest starts running the day after the payment deadline.

Miss the filing deadline and the penalty is 5% of the unpaid tax plus 1% for each full month the return is late, up to 12 months. If the CRA already issued a late-filing demand and you were late again in one of the three prior years, that jumps to 10% plus 2% per month for up to 20 months. Filing on time — even when you can't pay in full — is almost always the cheaper move.

The Small Business Deduction: Your Best CCPC Benefit

The small business deduction (SBD) is the single biggest reason incorporation pays off for an active business. It drops the federal corporate rate on the first $500,000 of active business income from 15% down to 9%. Ontario recently cut its small-business rate to 2.2% (effective July 1, 2026), so a qualifying Ontario CCPC pays roughly 11.2% combined on that income once the new rate is fully in effect — versus the general corporate rate of about 26.5% in Ontario on income above the limit. (Corporations whose fiscal year straddles the July 1 change blend the old and new provincial rates for that year.)

To claim it, your corporation has to be a CCPC throughout the year. A few things chip away at the deduction to watch for:

The $500,000 business limit is shared across associated corporations, so if you control more than one company you split one limit, not several. The limit also grinds down once a CCPC group has more than $50,000 of passive investment income in the prior year (fully eliminated at $150,000), and it's reduced for larger corporations based on taxable capital. If any of that describes you, the SBD is worth planning around well before year-end.

Common T2 Deductions for Small Business

Your corporation can deduct any reasonable expense incurred to earn income. The everyday ones:

Salaries and wages (including your own T4 pay), rent and utilities, professional and accounting fees, business insurance, advertising and marketing, software and subscriptions, bank and merchant fees, and the business-use portion of vehicle costs. Capital assets — equipment, computers, furniture, a company vehicle — aren't fully written off the year you buy them; instead you claim capital cost allowance (CCA), depreciating them over time at set rates.

If you run the corporation from a dedicated space in your home, you can deduct a reasonable share of home costs (heat, hydro, property tax, insurance, mortgage interest or rent) based on the square footage used for the business. Note the temporary $2-a-day flat-rate home-office method from the pandemic years is gone — for the 2025 tax year and onward there's no shortcut, so keep the receipts and use the detailed calculation.

One deduction worth timing deliberately: enhanced first-year CCA. Recent federal measures let businesses accelerate write-offs on certain assets — for example, immediate expensing on qualifying manufacturing and processing or clean-energy equipment, and boosted first-year rates on many other capital purchases. The specific classes, rates, and phase-out dates shift year to year, so confirm what applies to a given purchase before you count on the deduction.

Salary vs. Dividends: How to Pay Yourself

Once your corporation is profitable, the next question is how to get money into your own pocket. There's no universal right answer — it depends on your income level, cash-flow needs, and long-term plans.

Salary is a deductible expense to the corporation, so it lowers corporate tax. It also creates RRSP contribution room, counts toward CPP, and gives you predictable pay stubs that lenders like to see. The trade-off is CPP: you pay both the employee and employer halves.

Dividends aren't deductible to the corporation, but they're taxed more lightly in your hands thanks to the dividend tax credit, and there's no CPP. The downside is no RRSP room and no CPP benefits building for retirement. Many owner-managers use a blend — enough salary to max RRSP room and hit CPP, with dividends on top.

We model both paths for every incorporated client, because the "cheapest" option on paper isn't always the right one once RRSPs, CPP, and future plans are in the picture.

Corporate Tax Instalments: Do You Have to Pay Quarterly?

If your corporation owes more than $3,000 of federal tax (and, in provinces where it applies, provincial tax) in the current or prior year, the CRA generally expects instalments through the year instead of one lump sum. Most small CCPCs pay quarterly; some qualify to pay quarterly with a longer schedule, while other corporations pay monthly.

The CRA sends instalment reminders, but they're based on prior-year figures and can be off if your income changed. Underpay and you'll owe instalment interest — and, if it's large enough, an additional instalment penalty. It's worth a quick check each quarter rather than a surprise at year-end.

The T2 rewards businesses that plan ahead of their year-end rather than scrambling after it.
