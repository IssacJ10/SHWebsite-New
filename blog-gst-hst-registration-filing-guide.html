GST/HST Registration and Filing Guide (2026)

GST and HST are the same federal sales tax wearing two hats: a straight 5% GST in provinces that never harmonized, and a combined 13% or 15% HST in the ones that did. If you sell taxable goods or services in Canada, the moment your revenue crosses a certain line you're legally on the hook to register, charge tax, and remit it to the CRA. This 2026 guide walks you through when you have to register, how to actually do it, which rate to charge, how often you'll file, and how to claw back the tax you paid on your own expenses.

Who Has to Register: The $30,000 Small-Supplier Threshold

The CRA calls you a "small supplier" — and lets you skip GST/HST registration — until your worldwide taxable revenue crosses $30,000. That figure counts your gross taxable and zero-rated sales, not your profit, and it isn't measured over a tidy calendar year. You blow past the threshold either way:

In a single calendar quarter. If your taxable revenue tops $30,000 in one quarter alone, you stop being a small supplier immediately. Your first taxable sale after that point already needs to have GST/HST on it, and you have to register right away — effective the date of that sale.

Over four consecutive calendar quarters. More often, you creep over the line gradually. If your total taxable revenue exceeds $30,000 across any four consecutive quarters (rolling, not just January-to-December), you stop being a small supplier one month after the end of that fourth quarter. You then have 29 days from that effective date to register, and you must charge tax on supplies made on or after it.

The practical takeaway: watch your trailing 12-month revenue, not the calendar. Miss the deadline and the CRA can still assess you for the tax you should have collected — meaning it comes out of your own pocket if you never billed your customers for it.

Voluntary Registration: When It Pays to Register Early

Even if you're comfortably under $30,000, you're allowed to register voluntarily — and sometimes it's the smarter move. Once you're registered you can claim Input Tax Credits (ITCs) to recover the GST/HST you pay on business purchases, which is a real cash refund if your input tax exceeds the tax you collect. That's common in the early, investment-heavy stage of a business, or if most of your customers are themselves GST/HST-registered and don't care that you add tax to their invoice. The trade-off: you take on the paperwork of charging, filing, and remitting from day one. If you sell mainly to individual consumers, adding tax before you're required to can just make you 5-13% more expensive than a competitor who hasn't registered yet.

How to Register: Getting Your Business Number and RT Account

Registration is free and, for most people, quick. The main route is CRA's Business Registration Online (BRO):

1. Get a Business Number (BN). If you don't already have one, BRO issues the nine-digit BN that identifies your business to the CRA. Sole proprietors register under their own name; incorporated businesses register under the corporation.

2. Add a GST/HST program account (the "RT" account). Your GST/HST account is a program account bolted onto the BN — it shows up as your BN followed by "RT0001." This is the account you'll use to file returns and remit tax.

3. Set your effective date and reporting period. During registration you'll confirm the date you became a registrant (get this right — it's the date you started having to charge tax) and your filing frequency. You can also register by phone or by mailing Form RC1, but online is fastest.

Once you're registered, start showing GST/HST on your invoices and include your GST/HST number so your customers can claim their own ITCs.

GST vs. HST Rates Across Canada in 2026

The rate you charge depends on where the supply is made, not where your office is. In 2026 the rates are: 5% GST in Alberta, British Columbia, Saskatchewan, Manitoba, Quebec, and the three territories (with provincial sales tax stacked separately in some of those); 13% HST in Ontario; and 15% HST in New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, Prince Edward Island, and Newfoundland and Labrador.

If you sell across provincial lines — which most remote and online businesses now do — the "place of supply" rules decide which province's rate applies, and it's usually driven by where your customer or the property is located. Get it wrong and you either over-charge a customer or under-remit to the CRA, so it's worth confirming the rate for each province you sell into.

Input Tax Credits (ITCs): Getting Your Money Back

ITCs are the reason registration isn't purely a cost. They let you recover the GST/HST you paid on purchases used in your commercial activities, netting it against the tax you collected. If you paid more input tax than you collected in a period, the difference comes back to you as a refund.

To claim an ITC you need proper documentation on file: the supplier's name, their GST/HST number, the date, the amount paid, and the tax charged. For larger purchases the CRA expects more detail, so keep the actual invoices, not just credit-card slips. Common ITC-eligible expenses include office supplies, equipment, software and professional services, the business portion of vehicle expenses, rent, and utilities.

Two limits catch people out. First, meals and entertainment: you can generally claim only 50% of the GST/HST on those costs, mirroring the 50% income-tax deduction limit. Second, you can only claim the business-use share of anything used partly for personal purposes. Keep the records — the ITC is only as good as the paper trail behind it.

Filing Frequencies and Due Dates: Annual, Quarterly, or Monthly

The CRA assigns your reporting period based on your annual taxable supplies. Businesses with $1.5 million or less can file annually; those between $1.5 million and $6 million file quarterly; and those over $6 million file monthly. You can always elect to file more often than required — many small businesses choose quarterly so ITC refunds come back sooner and the tax they've collected doesn't sit in the account for a full year.

Due dates depend on your period. For monthly and quarterly filers, both the return and the payment are due one month after the end of the reporting period. Annual filers generally have three months after their fiscal year-end to file and pay — but if you're an annual filer with a December 31 year-end and business income on your personal return, your return is due June 15 while any balance owing is still due April 30. Late filing and late payment both carry penalties and interest, so diarize the dates even in a quarter where you're claiming a refund.

One more thing worth planning for: the CRA can require quarterly or annual filers to make instalment payments during the year if your net tax hits certain levels, so don't be surprised by an instalment notice as you grow.
