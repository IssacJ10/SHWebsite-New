How to File Your T1 Tax Return in Canada (2026): A Step-by-Step Guide

Filing your T1 return doesn't have to be stressful — most of the work is gathering the right slips before you sit down. This guide walks you through how to file your T1 tax return in Canada in 2026, step by step: what you need, how to send it to the CRA, when it's due, and how long a refund takes. We're covering the 2025 tax year, the return most Canadians file in the spring of 2026.

Do You Need to File a T1 Return?

You have to file if you owe tax for the year, if the CRA sent you a request to file, or if you sold capital property (a rental, a cottage, stocks) — and starting a couple of years back, the CRA wants a return even when you sell your principal residence, even though the gain is usually exempt. You'll also want to file if you're owed a refund.

Here's the part people skip: even with little or no income, filing is usually worth it. Your benefit and credit payments — the GST/HST credit, the Canada Carbon Rebate where it still applies, the Canada Child Benefit, and most provincial programs — are recalculated from your return every year. Miss the filing and those payments can stop. Canadian residents report their worldwide income; non-residents with Canadian-source income may need to file too.

Step 1: Confirm Your SIN and Set Up CRA My Account

You can't file without a valid Social Insurance Number, so have it handy. If you don't already have CRA My Account, register now rather than at the deadline — it can take a few days to receive the security code by mail. My Account is where you'll find your slips through Auto-fill my return, check your RRSP and TFSA room, see your notice of assessment, and — importantly — set up direct deposit so any refund lands in your bank account instead of a mailed cheque.

Step 2: Gather Your Slips and Receipts

Most of filing is just collecting paper. Issuers have until the end of February to send most slips, so it's worth waiting until early March so nothing arrives after you've filed. Common income slips include the T4 (employment), T4A (pension, self-employed commissions, and other income), T5 (investment income), T3 (trust and mutual-fund income), T4E (EI benefits), T5007 (social assistance and workers' comp), and the T2202 (tuition).

On the receipts side, pull together your RRSP contribution receipts (including any from the first 60 days of 2026, which count toward the 2025 year), medical expenses, charitable donation receipts, childcare costs, union or professional dues, and — if you're self-employed — a tidy summary of your business income and expenses for your T2125. A quick tip: run Auto-fill my return first, then check the downloaded slips against your own paper. Auto-fill is a great head start, but late-filed slips don't always show up, and the return is still your responsibility.

Step 3: Complete the T1 and Its Schedules

The T1 General is the main return, but most of the real work happens on the schedules that feed into it — your provincial or territorial form (for Ontario filers, the ON428), Schedule 3 for capital gains, Schedule 7 for RRSP contributions and the Home Buyers' Plan, Schedule 9 for donations, and the T2125 statement of business or professional activities if you're self-employed. Certified tax software fills these in for you as you answer questions, so you rarely touch the raw forms — but it helps to know what's driving your result.

Two credits worth flagging because people leave money on the table: if you worked from home, the temporary $2-a-day flat-rate method is gone — to claim home-office costs now you use the detailed method — a signed T2200 from your employer, with your expenses reported on Form T777 — or, if you're self-employed, the business-use-of-home section of the T2125. Also don't overlook the disability tax credit, the Canada Workers Benefit, medical expenses, and tuition carried forward from prior years.

Step 4: File by NETFILE, EFILE, or Paper

You have three ways to send the return. NETFILE lets you file it yourself directly to the CRA using certified software — the CRA publishes the list of NETFILE-certified products each year, and you should stick to one on that list. EFILE is when a registered tax preparer like SH Elevate transmits it for you. Paper is still accepted, but it's slow: expect roughly eight weeks or more to process, versus about two weeks for an electronic return with direct deposit. The CRA typically opens NETFILE in late February, so early filers often wait a couple of weeks for the system to go live.

Filing Deadlines and Late-Filing Penalties for 2026

For most people, the deadline to file your 2025 return is April 30, 2026. If you or your spouse or common-law partner ran a business, you both get until June 15, 2026 to file — but here's the catch that trips people up: any balance you owe is still due April 30, 2026. The extra time is for the paperwork, not the payment.

File late with a balance owing and the penalty is 5% of that balance, plus another 1% for every full month the return is late, up to 12 months. On top of the penalty, the CRA charges compound daily interest on what you owe. If you can't pay in full, file on time anyway — that alone spares you the 5% + 1% penalty, and you can arrange to pay the balance over time.

After You File: Refunds and Your Notice of Assessment

Once your return is accepted, the CRA issues a notice of assessment confirming its version of your numbers. With NETFILE or EFILE and direct deposit, refunds often arrive in around two weeks; paper returns and mailed cheques take considerably longer. Read the assessment when it arrives — it flags your remaining RRSP room, any changes the CRA made, and instalment reminders if you owe enough to trigger them for next year. Keep your slips and receipts for six years in case the CRA asks to see them.
