How to Incorporate a Business in Ontario: A 2026 Step-by-Step Guide

Learning how to incorporate a business in Ontario in 2026 comes down to a handful of decisions and a few hundred dollars in government fees. A corporation gives you limited liability protection, access to the small business tax rate (roughly 12.2% combined federal and Ontario on the first $500,000 of active business income), and a cleaner structure for splitting income and raising capital. This guide walks you through the choice between federal and provincial, what each actually costs, and the paperwork that follows — start to finish.

Federal vs. Ontario Incorporation: Which Should You Choose?

You have two paths. You can incorporate provincially through the Ontario Business Registry, or federally through Corporations Canada under the CBCA. Both give you a valid corporation and limited liability — the difference is name protection, cost, and where you're allowed to carry on business under that name.

Provincial incorporation protects your corporate name only within Ontario and keeps the process simple. Federal incorporation gives you name rights across the entire country, but a federal corporation still has to register extra-provincially in Ontario before it can operate here — an extra filing that adds cost.

Here's the plain-language rule of thumb: if you'll operate mainly in Ontario, incorporate provincially — it's cheaper and there's less to maintain. If you plan to do business in more than one province, want your name protected nationally, or expect to raise outside capital, go federal. When in doubt, most Ontario small businesses start provincial and can always continue federally later if they expand.

What Does It Cost to Incorporate in Ontario in 2026?

Government fees change, so treat these as approximate figures as of 2026 and confirm the current amounts before you file. That said, here's roughly what you're looking at:

Ontario provincial (≈ $380): about $300 for the Articles of Incorporation filed online, plus about $80 for the required Ontario-biased NUANS name search if you want a named corporation.

Federal (≈ $530–$545): about $200 for the CBCA Articles of Incorporation filed online through Corporations Canada, plus about $13.80 for the NUANS name search, plus about $330 to register the federal corporation extra-provincially in Ontario so it can operate here.

So a straightforward Ontario-only company lands near $380, while going federal runs closer to $530–$545 once the mandatory Ontario registration is included. These are government charges only — they don't include professional fees to set up your share structure, minute book, and CRA accounts correctly.

Step 1: NUANS Name Search (or Go Numbered)

If you want a named corporation — say, "Maple Grove Consulting Inc." — you first run a NUANS report to confirm your proposed name doesn't clash with an existing corporate name or trademark. A federal NUANS report costs about $13.80 and stays valid for 90 days; Ontario requires its own Ontario-biased NUANS search (around $80 through a service provider) for provincial filings.

Prefer to skip this entirely? Choose a numbered company. Ontario simply assigns you something like "1234567 Ontario Inc.," with no name search needed. You can always register a business name or trade name to operate under later, which is a common approach for holding companies.

Step 2: File Your Articles of Incorporation

The Articles of Incorporation are the founding document. You file them with the Ontario Business Registry for a provincial corporation, or with Corporations Canada for a federal one. In the articles you set out the corporation's name, its registered office, the share structure and any transfer restrictions, and the first directors.

Processing is quick when you file online — typically around five business days, sometimes faster. Get the share structure right at this stage: it's far cheaper to set up thoughtful share classes now than to reorganize them later when you want to split income or bring in an investor.

Step 3: Get Your Business Number and CRA Program Accounts

Once you're incorporated, the Canada Revenue Agency issues your corporation a nine-digit Business Number (BN). On top of that, you register the specific program accounts your business needs:

RC — Corporate income tax: almost every incorporated business needs this to file its annual T2 return.
RT — GST/HST: required once your taxable revenue crosses $30,000 over four consecutive quarters, and often worth registering voluntarily before then to recover input tax credits.
RP — Payroll: needed as soon as you pay yourself a salary or hire employees, so you can remit source deductions.
RM — Import/export: only if you'll be moving goods across the border.

Federal incorporations are often assigned a BN automatically, but you still have to open the individual program accounts that apply to you.

Ongoing Compliance After You Incorporate

Incorporating is the start, not the finish. Each year you'll file a T2 corporate return within six months of your fiscal year-end (with any tax owing generally due two or three months after year-end), file your annual return to keep the corporation in good standing, maintain a corporate minute book, hold and document annual shareholder meetings, and keep clean records. Miss the annual return and the registry can eventually dissolve your corporation — a headache that's entirely avoidable with a simple calendar reminder.
