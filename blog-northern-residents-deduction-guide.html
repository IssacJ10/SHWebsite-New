Northern Residents Deduction (2026): Who Qualifies and How to Claim It

If you live somewhere remote in Canada, the Northern Residents Deduction is one of the most valuable write-offs you can claim — and one people miss most often. It rewards you simply for living in the North: no receipts needed for the residency part, and often a few thousand dollars off your taxable income. The catch is knowing which zone you're in and hitting the six-month rule. We see this regularly with clients in Thunder Bay, Timmins, Sudbury, Sault Ste. Marie, Corner Brook, and Grande Prairie, so here's exactly how the Northern Residents Deduction works for the 2025 tax year (the return you file by April 30, 2026) and beyond.

Who Qualifies for the Northern Residents Deduction?

There's really one gate to clear: you must have lived in a prescribed zone throughout a continuous period of at least six consecutive months. That period can straddle two tax years — so if you moved north in, say, September, you may still qualify once you've hit the six-month mark, and you claim for the months that fall in the tax year. You don't have to be there on December 31; you need the continuous six months.

Canada has two prescribed zones, and which one you're in decides how much you get:

  • Prescribed Northern Zone (Zone A) — full deduction. This covers the three territories and the far-northern parts of most provinces: much of Northern Ontario, northern Alberta, Saskatchewan, Manitoba, Quebec and B.C., plus most of Newfoundland and Labrador.
  • Prescribed Intermediate Zone (Zone B) — half the deduction. These are the "in-between" areas a notch south of Zone A, including communities like Sudbury and Sault Ste. Marie.

Don't guess your zone — a town and its neighbour can fall on opposite sides of the line. The CRA publishes a searchable list of prescribed zones by province and territory (Publication T4039), and that's the source of truth for a claim.

The deduction itself comes in two parts: a residency amount (for simply living there) and a travel benefits amount (for trips your employer helped pay for). You can claim one or both.

Part 1: The Residency Amount

The residency amount is a flat rate for every day you lived in a prescribed zone during the year. In the Northern Zone the basic residency amount is $11.00 per day; in the Intermediate Zone it's half that, $5.50 per day. Over a full year that works out to roughly $4,015 in the North and about $2,008 in the intermediate zone.

Here's the part people leave on the table: there's a basic residency amount and an additional residency amount of the same size. If you maintained and lived in a dwelling in the zone and no one else in that household is claiming the basic amount for those days, you can claim the additional amount too — effectively doubling your deduction (up to about $8,030 a year in the North). If two people in the same home each want to claim, only one of you can take the additional amount, so it's usually worth deciding who claims what before you file.

Part 2: The Travel Benefits Amount

This second part only applies if your employer gave you taxable travel benefits — a travel allowance or trips that showed up as a taxable benefit on your T4 or T4A. If that's you, you can deduct eligible travel expenses for personal or medical trips, generally for up to two non-medical trips per person in the household each year (medical trips aren't capped at two).

For each trip your deduction is the lowest of three numbers: the taxable travel benefit your employer reported, your actual travel costs, and the lowest return airfare available at the time of the trip between your community and the nearest designated city. Those designated cities are set by the CRA — for Thunder Bay residents the nearest is typically Winnipeg or Toronto; for Corner Brook, Halifax or St. John's. Keep the boarding passes, receipts and any airfare quotes, because the airfare figure is the one the CRA looks at most closely.

How to Claim the Northern Residents Deduction on Your T1

You claim the deduction with Form T2222 (Northern Residents Deductions) and carry the total to Line 25500 of your T1 return. The steps are straightforward once your records are in order:

  1. Confirm your community is in a prescribed zone (and which one) using the CRA's list.
  2. Count your residency days in the zone and decide who in the household claims the basic and additional amounts.
  3. If you had employer travel benefits, work out the lowest-of-three figure for each eligible trip.
  4. Total everything on Form T2222 and enter the amount on Line 25500.

On record-keeping: you don't file receipts with your return, but the CRA can ask for them afterward. Hold on to proof of where you lived and for how long, your T4/T4A slips showing any travel benefit, and your travel receipts and airfare quotes — for at least six years, the standard CRA retention window.

The Northern Residents Deduction is one of those claims where a small detail — the wrong zone, a missed additional amount, or a travel figure that doesn't hold up — can cost you real money or invite a review.
