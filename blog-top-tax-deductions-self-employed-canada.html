Self-Employed Tax Deductions in Canada: Your 2026 Checklist

If you freelance, contract, consult, or drive for an app, you get to claim expenses a salaried employee never can — and most people leave real money on the table simply because they don't know what qualifies. This is a practical checklist of the self-employed tax deductions in Canada worth tracking in 2026. Everything below flows through one form: the T2125 (Statement of Business or Professional Activities), which you file with your personal T1 return. For the 2025 tax year, self-employed people had until June 15, 2026 to file — but any balance owing was still due April 30, 2026, so filing late doesn't buy you extra time to pay.

The rule that ties it all together: T2125

Every business expense you claim lands on the T2125 and reduces your net self-employment income. That matters twice over: it lowers your income tax, and because CPP for the self-employed is calculated on that same net figure, legitimate deductions shrink your CPP bill too. So a dollar of genuine business expense saves you more than a dollar of employment income would. The catch is "genuine" — the expense has to be reasonable and incurred to earn income, and you need a record to back it up.

1. Home office — detailed method only

If you regularly use part of your home to run your business, you can deduct that portion of your rent or mortgage interest, property tax, utilities, home insurance, and maintenance. Work out your business-use percentage by area (say, a 120 sq ft office in a 1,200 sq ft home is 10%) and apply it to those costs.

One important update: the temporary $2/day flat-rate shortcut that existed during the pandemic is gone. For 2026 you use the detailed method — you total your actual home costs and claim the business-use share. Keep your bills and a note of the square footage. Your home-office claim also can't create or increase a business loss, but any unused amount carries forward to a future year.

2. Vehicle and mileage

Drive for work? You can deduct the business-use share of fuel, insurance, repairs, licence and registration, lease payments (or capital cost allowance if you own), and interest on a car loan. The number that makes or breaks this claim is your logbook: track business kilometres versus total kilometres for the year, and that ratio is your deductible percentage. Parking and the 407 toll for a business trip are fully deductible on top of that.

3. Meals and entertainment (50%)

Taking a client for coffee or lunch, or entertaining a business contact, is deductible — but only at 50% of the cost, and the same 50% cap applies to the tip and tax. Keep the receipt and jot down who you met and why. (Grabbing lunch by yourself between jobs is not deductible — that's a personal expense.)

4. Advertising and marketing

Website hosting, your domain, Google and social ads, printed cards, and design work are fully deductible. There is one limit worth knowing: advertising placed with foreign online platforms is generally still claimable, but ad buys in foreign newspapers or on foreign broadcasters aimed at a Canadian market usually aren't — a spot most sole proprietors never hit, but worth a check if you advertise abroad.

5. Professional fees and bank charges

Your accountant's fee, bookkeeping, and legal fees tied to the business are deductible — including what you pay to have this very return prepared. So are the monthly fees, transaction charges, and interest on a dedicated business bank account or credit card. Opening a separate account for the business is the single easiest thing you can do to make this clean at tax time.

6. Software, subscriptions, and equipment

Your SaaS stack — accounting software, design tools, cloud storage, project management, the AI subscription you actually use for work — is deductible. Smaller tools and supplies are expensed in the year you buy them. Bigger purchases like a laptop or camera are usually claimed over several years through capital cost allowance (CCA), though certain assets qualify for accelerated or immediate write-offs. When in doubt on a big-ticket item, ask before you file.

7. Phone, internet, and office supplies

Claim the business-use portion of your cell phone and home internet — if half your data is work, claim half. Ordinary office supplies (paper, ink, postage, that box of pens) are fully deductible in the year you buy them.

8. Insurance

Commercial and professional liability insurance, errors-and-omissions coverage, and other business insurance premiums are deductible. Note that your personal life insurance generally is not — this is about coverage tied to the business.

9. CPP for the self-employed in 2026

Here's the part employees don't deal with: as your own boss you pay both halves of CPP. For 2026 the self-employed contribution rate is 11.9% on pensionable earnings, which works out to a maximum of roughly $8,460.90 on first-tier earnings, plus the second additional CPP (CPP2) on income above the first ceiling. It's a real cost — but you get relief on it. You can deduct the employer half of your CPP on your return, and you claim a credit for the employee half, so it isn't taxed twice. It's built into your return automatically, but it's why setting money aside through the year matters.

10. Keep your records for six years

This is the deduction insurance policy. The CRA can ask you to prove any claim, and the rule is that you keep your receipts, logbooks, invoices, and bank statements for six years from the end of the tax year they relate to. No receipt, no deduction if you're audited — so a simple habit of scanning receipts and logging mileage as you go is worth more than any single line above.

The goal isn't to claim everything imaginable — it's to claim everything you're genuinely entitled to, cleanly enough that it holds up.
