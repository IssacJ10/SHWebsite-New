T2 Corporate Tax Returns

The T2 corporate tax return, done right.

Every incorporated business in Canada has to file a T2 corporate tax return each year, even in a year with no income. We prepare that return end to end — from startups filing their first year to established corporations with more moving parts — and look for the deductions and credits that lower what your company actually owes.

We start from your financial statements, tie them out to your bookkeeping, and build the schedules the CRA expects. If your books aren't ready, we can clean them up first so the return rests on numbers you can defend.

  • ✓ Income and expenses reconciled to your statements
  • ✓ Capital cost allowance and available tax credits applied
  • ✓ Electronic filing to the CRA on your business number

What a T2 return covers

  • Active business income reported and reconciled to your books
  • Deductible expenses with capital cost allowance schedules
  • Small business deduction and available corporate credits
  • Dividends paid and capital gains treatment
  • GIFI financial-statement data (Schedule 100/125)
  • Instalment planning and balance-due timing

Dividend Strategy

Salary, bonus, or dividend? We run the numbers.

How you pay yourself changes your total tax bill. Salary builds RRSP room and CPP; dividends skip CPP but come from after-tax corporate profit. There's rarely one right answer, so we compare the options against your personal bracket and your company's rate before you decide.

Salary vs. Dividend Analysis

We compare a salary, a dividend, or a mix of both against your tax bracket, your RRSP goals, and how much the business needs to keep on hand.

  • • CPP contributions weighed against RRSP room
  • • How corporate and personal tax integrate
  • • Income-splitting options within the TOSI rules
  • • Dividend gross-up and dividend tax credit math

Multi-Shareholder Planning

Your T2 return is due six months after the end of your corporation's fiscal year — so a December 31 year-end means a June 30 filing deadline. Any tax you owe is usually due sooner, within two or three months of year-end depending on your corporation, so we plan for the payment date as well as the filing date.
It depends. A salary is a deductible expense for the company, builds RRSP room, and requires CPP contributions and payroll remittances. Dividends come out of after-tax corporate profit, skip CPP, and are taxed differently in your hands. We compare the two — or a mix — against your personal bracket before you decide.
The small business deduction applies to a Canadian-controlled private corporation (CCPC) and lowers the federal tax rate on the first $500,000 of active business income. Investment income and larger corporations can grind that limit down. We check where your corporation stands and structure things to keep as much income as possible under the limit.
Integration is the idea that a dollar of business income should carry roughly the same total tax whether you take it as salary or as a dividend. In practice it's never perfect, and the small gaps between rates are exactly where salary-vs-dividend planning earns its keep for owners.
